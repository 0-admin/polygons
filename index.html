I'm not biased against squares. I hang out with lots of squares! Like all shapes, I'm really only uncomfortable when an overwhelming majority of the shapes around me are different. I mean, I don't run screaming, I'm not shapist or anything, but given the opportunity to move, I think we'd all rather be in a 50/50 sort of situation.

50/50 is pretty ideal for all shapes in shapeland, and if it's 60/40 and I'm in the minority, well, I don't mind! Better than being in a boring neighborhood of all triangles! All shapes are basically the same. We only want to move if less than a third (33%) of our neighbors are like us. If that's bias, well, surely it's a minor one.

If everyone were as unbiased as I was, wouldn't everything be fine?

What is up with this shape separation? My people are good shapes, nice shapes. So why is it that even when every individual seems like a model citizen, with just a slight preference to move only if we're overwhelmingly surrounded by shapes unlike us, why does shape life end up so boring?

Even with little individual bias, you can end up with massive institutional bias.

Equality is an unstable equilibrium. It breaks at the slightest disturbance. Perhaps we could teach shapes to have less bias. Perhaps it's wrong of me to notice when I'm surrounded by squares, and wrong that squares who visit our triangle-dominated areas never seem to want to stay. What if we could teach all shapes to have zero preference for the shape of their neighbors? Or what if we just gave up, and decided to stick with our own kind?

Our cute, adorable segregation simulator is based off the work of Nobel Prize-winning game theorist, Thomas Schelling. Specifically, his mathematical model of bias found in Dynamic Models of Segregation. We built on top of his model, by showing how adding a small cultural demand for diversity can help desegregate a neighbourhood. In other words, we gave his model a happy ending.

Schelling's model is a convincing demonstration of how innocent-seeming rules can create very undesirable outcomes, but of course real-life situations are more complicated. You might enjoy taking a closer look at real data in reference to Schelling's model. W.A.V. Clark's 1991 paper Residential Preferences and Neighborhood Racial Segregation: A Test of the Schelling Segregation Model has a lot of interesting data in it.

Schelling's model explores one small aspect of how tiny bias can have large accumulative effects, but there's other models that focus on other aspects. Male-Female Differences: A Computer Simulation shows how extremely small differences in the perception of men and women's work can have huge effects. The Petrie Multiplier explores how small negative actions of a few bad apples can become multiplied by certain situations.

The takeaway from our variant of Schelling's simulator is that demanding a single bit of diversity in your spaces makes a huge difference overall. For a real-world application of this idea, see Plz Diversify Your Panel, an initiative where overrepresented speakers refuse invitations to speak on panels that don't include diverse representation.
